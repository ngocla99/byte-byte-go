You’re Not Getting Promoted Because You’re Too Good At Your Job

Wondering why you didn’t get promoted? Here’s how being too good at execution can actually hold you back.

Oct 19
 
READ IN APP
 

I watched a senior engineer get passed over for staff last month.

He was the best debugger on the team. Everyone came to him when things broke. He’d fixed three critical production incidents in the last quarter alone.

His manager told him: “You’re incredibly valuable, but you’re not ready for staff yet.”

He was confused. So was I, at first.

Then I realized: He was too busy being a hero to multiply his impact.

This is the problem with getting really good at senior engineering. You optimize for the wrong things.

Let me show you what I mean.

The Three Things That Actually Matter (And Why You’re Ignoring Them)

Most engineers think promotions are about getting better at what you already do.

They’re not.

They’re about doing a different job.

Here’s what that actually looks like:

1. You’re Still Solving Problems Instead of Preventing Them

You’re the hero who swoops in at 11pm to fix the outage. You debug the gnarliest production issues. Everyone comes to you when things break.

You think this proves you’re essential.

It proves you’re reactive.

The Hero Complex

I worked with a senior engineer, let’s call him Mike, who was legendary for fixing production issues. 2am database deadlock? Mike’s on it. Weird cache inconsistency? Mike figures it out in 20 minutes.

His performance review said: “Critical incident responder. Saves the day repeatedly.”

He didn’t get promoted.

Meanwhile, Sarah, who honestly wasn’t as strong technically, got the staff role.

Why?

Sarah spent a month after an incident building tooling that detected the same class of problems before they became incidents. She wrote documentation on how to diagnose them. She ran a workshop teaching six engineers how the database connection pooling worked.

The next time it happened, two different engineers fixed it in 10 minutes without waking anyone up.

Mike was solving the same problem over and over. Sarah made the problem go away.

Here’s what actually gets promoted: The engineer who looks at those three outages and says: “Why do we keep having database connection pool exhaustion? Let me add monitoring, update our capacity planning doc, and teach the team how to spot this early.”

The shift you need to make:

Stop being the best debugger. Start being the person who makes debugging unnecessary.

That looks like:

  • Writing the runbook while fixing the issue

  • Building the dashboard that spots problems before they’re outages

  • Teaching two other people how to handle it next time

Promoted engineers multiply their impact. You’re still dividing yours by the number of fires you can personally fight.

2. You’re Optimizing For Your Team, Not The Company

You made your team’s deployment pipeline amazing. Your microservice has perfect test coverage. Your API is beautifully designed.

Meanwhile, the mobile team is blocked because your service doesn’t expose the data they need. Product can’t launch the feature because your API requires three round trips.

You think shipping quality code is enough.

It’s not.

The Rewrite Nobody Needed

I watched an engineer spend four months rewriting a service “the right way.” Clean architecture. Proper abstractions. Beautiful code.

The company missed a market opportunity because that service was on the critical path for a partnership deal.

A competitor shipped a scrappier version in six weeks and signed the partner first.

His manager told him: “Your code is excellent. But we needed that feature in Q2, not Q3.”

He didn’t get promoted that cycle.

Here’s what hurts: he was right about the technical debt. The old service was a mess. But he optimized for code quality when the company needed speed.

The engineer who got promoted instead? She shipped the minimum viable version in three weeks. It had TODO comments and some copy-pasted code. It also unblocked three other teams and closed a seven-figure deal.

She cleaned up the tech debt after the deadline.

What actually gets promoted: The engineer who asks: “What’s the company trying to do this quarter?” and then figures out how their work unblocks it.

The shift you need to make:

Stop optimizing for elegance. Start optimizing for business impact.

That looks like:

  • Talking to product before you build

  • Asking other teams what they need from you

  • Sometimes shipping the ugly-but-fast solution that unblocks five other teams

Perfect code that ships too late is worth less than messy code that ships on time.

3. You Think Visibility Is Politics

You ship a complex feature. It works great. Nobody knows you built it.

You think good work speaks for itself.

It doesn’t.

Meanwhile, someone else writes a two-paragraph Slack post about a simple refactor and suddenly everyone thinks they’re crushing it.

You think this is unfair. You think they’re “playing politics.”

Here’s the truth: If your manager doesn’t know what you did, it didn’t happen.

The Silent Contributor

I knew an engineer who built the entire observability platform for a 50-person engineering org. Metrics, logs, traces, alerting. The whole thing.

He got passed over for promotion.

Why? His manager didn’t know he’d done it.

The engineer thought: “Obviously everyone knows. They use it every day.”

But his manager’s world is back-to-back meetings. She knew the observability platform existed. She didn’t know who built it, how long it took, or what problems it solved.

When promotion time came, she couldn’t make the case for him because she didn’t have the data.

After he finally wrote it up, six months later, she said: “I had no idea you did all of this. Why didn’t you tell me?”

He thought sharing his work was bragging.

What actually gets promoted: The engineer who understands that documentation and communication aren’t someone else’s job.

The shift you need to make:

That looks like:

  • Writing a post-mortem after a launch (what worked, what didn’t, what we learned)

  • Sharing a weekly update with your manager (what you shipped, what’s blocked, what’s next)

  • Presenting your work at team meetings, not just merging the PR

You don’t need to brag. You need to communicate impact.

The Template That Actually Works

Here’s the formula: “I did X, which means Y can now happen, which helps the company do Z.”

Bad: “I refactored the payment service.”

Good: “I refactored the payment service to handle 3x load, which unblocks international expansion, which gets us to our Q4 revenue target.”

Bad: “Fixed the bug in checkout flow.”

Good: “Fixed the checkout race condition that was causing 2% of orders to fail. Estimated recovery: $50k MRR. Added tests and monitoring so we catch similar issues before they hit production.”

See the difference?

One sounds like you closed a ticket. The other sounds like you moved the business forward.

The Pattern Nobody Tells You

Notice what all three have in common?

They’re about changing your frame of reference.

Junior engineers solve tickets
Senior engineers solve problems
Staff engineers solve problems at scale

You get promoted when you stop thinking like an individual contributor and start thinking like a force multiplier.

That doesn’t mean you stop coding. It means every line of code you write should make ten other people more effective.

If you’re still measuring your impact by how much you shipped, you’re not ready for staff. You’re ready for another year as senior.

What This Actually Looks Like

Let me break down a real week for a staff engineer I work with:

Monday: Reviewed PRs, but also left comments explaining why certain patterns matter, turning code review into teaching moments.

Tuesday: Debugged a production issue, but spent an extra hour writing the runbook and updating the team wiki.

Wednesday: Joined product planning for next quarter. Asked: “Which of these initiatives are blocked on infrastructure?” Worked with management to Reprioritize roadmaps accordingly.

Thursday: Shipped a feature, then wrote a Slack post explaining the technical decisions and what other teams should know.

Friday: One-on-ones with three engineers, helping them think through their projects, not solving the problems for them.

Notice: He’s coding maybe 50% of the time. The rest is multiplying the impact of his code and other people’s code.

That’s what staff engineering looks like.

The Self-Assessment

Ask yourself:

On prevention:

  • When was the last time you fixed the same type of issue twice?

  • Do you have a list of “things we should really fix” that you never get to?

  • When something breaks, do you just fix it, or do you make sure it can’t break that way again?

On business impact:

  • Can you explain how your last three projects helped the company hit its goals?

  • Do you know what your CEO is worried about this quarter?

  • Have you talked to another team about what they need from you?

On visibility:

  • Does your skip-level manager know what you’re working on?

  • When was the last time you documented something you shipped?

  • Could someone else explain your impact, or is it all in your head?

If you’re uncomfortable with any of these questions, you found your gap.

What You Should Actually Do

These are all fixable. Start tomorrow.

Pick one:

Prevention: Next time you fix a bug, block 30 minutes to write the runbook or add monitoring.

Business impact: Schedule 30 minutes with your PM to ask: “What’s the company trying to do this quarter, and how can I help?”

Visibility: Send your manager a short update every Friday: three bullets on what you shipped, what’s blocked, what’s next.

Do one of these for a month. Then add the second. Then the third.

Six months from now, you’ll be a different engineer.

The technical skills you already have. The framing is what’s missing.

One More Thing

If you’re thinking: “But I don’t want to play politics, I just want to code”—I get it.

But here’s the thing: staff engineer is a different job than senior engineer.

If you want to keep being an individual contributor who writes great code and doesn’t deal with the rest, that’s valid. Some companies have principal engineer tracks for that.

But if you want the staff title, you’re signing up for a job that’s 50% coding and 50% multiplying the impact of other people’s code.

You don’t get promoted into the same job with a bigger title. You get promoted into a bigger job.

Make sure you actually want it.
