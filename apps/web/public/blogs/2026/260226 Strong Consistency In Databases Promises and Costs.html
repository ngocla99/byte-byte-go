Strong Consistency In Databases: Promises and Costs

If a database stores data on three servers in three different cities, and we write a new value, when exactly is that write “done”? Does it happen when the first server saves it? Or when all three have it? Or when two out of three confirm?

The answer to this question is quite important. Consider a simple bank transfer where we move $500 from savings to checking. We see the updated balance on our phone. But our partner, checking the same account from their laptop in another city, still sees the old balance. For a few seconds, the household has two different versions of the truth. For something similar to a like count on social media, that kind of temporary disagreement is harmless. For a bank balance, it’s a different story.

The guarantee that every reader sees the most recent write, no matter where or when they read, is what distributed systems engineers call strong consistency. It sounds straightforward. Making it work across machines, data centers, and continents is one of the hardest problems in distributed systems, because it requires those machines to coordinate, and coordination has a cost governed by physics.

In a previous article, we had looked at eventual consistency. In this article, we will look at what strong consistency actually means, how systems deliver it, and what it really costs.

What Strong Consistency Actually Promises

Strong consistency guarantees that from a client’s perspective, there appears to be only one copy of the data, and it’s always up to date.

The formal term for this is linearizability. In practical terms, it means that once a write completes, every subsequent read from any client, on any node, will return that write’s value or something even more recent. Operations appear to take effect instantaneously at some point between when they start and when they finish. This is the “C” in the CAP theorem, which states that a distributed system can provide at most two of three guarantees: Consistency, Availability, and Partition tolerance.

But there is a common misconception worth addressing.

Strong consistency does not mean all replicas are simultaneously updated. Under the hood, replicas can still receive updates at slightly different times. What the consensus protocol guarantees is that clients never observe this difference. The promise is about what we see, not about what’s physically happening on every machine at every instant. In a way, it’s like an illusion that there’s only one single copy of the data, and this illusion is maintained by careful coordination behind the scenes.

Why does this matter in practice?

Think about a concert ticket system. Two users, one in Stockholm and one in Berlin, both try to book the last available seat at the same time. With strong consistency, only one of them succeeds, and the other immediately sees the seat as taken. Without it, both might see the seat as available, both “book” it, and now we have an oversold concert and an angry customer.

Whenever two users see contradictory states for the same data creates a real problem. Whether it is bank balances, inventory counts, or seat reservations, strong consistency is the guarantee we need.

How Systems Deliver It: Consensus

So if strong consistency is the guarantee, what’s the mechanism that actually delivers it?

The answer is consensus. This is basically a protocol where machines actively agree on every write before it’s confirmed to the client. This is where the cost of strong consistency originates.

Raft and Paxos are the two dominant consensus algorithm families. They differ in mechanics but provide equivalent safety guarantees. At a high level, these algorithms work like this:

  • A cluster of nodes (typically 3 or 5) elects a leader.

  • All write requests go through the leader.

  • The leader proposes the write to the other nodes (followers).

  • Once a majority (called a quorum) acknowledges the write, it’s considered committed.

  • The leader confirms the write to the client.

That majority requirement is the key detail. With 3 nodes, 2 must acknowledge. With 5 nodes, 3 must acknowledge. This is what allows the system to tolerate failures. If one node goes down, the remaining nodes still form a majority and can keep operating.

However, the majority requirement is also what increases the cost. The write isn’t “done” until enough nodes respond. If all three nodes sit in the same data center, that coordination takes 1-5 milliseconds. However, if they’re spread across US-East, US-Central, and US-West, we’re waiting 30-40ms for the cross-region round-trip. And if the nodes are in New York, London, and Tokyo? That’s an even bigger wait time, because the signals between these nodes are ultimately limited by the speed of light through fiber optic cables.

The same protocol, the same algorithm, produces vastly different performance depending purely on physical distance.

A concrete example is etcd, the key-value store that backs Kubernetes and uses Raft consensus. Every update to cluster state, which pods are running, which services exist, and which configurations are active, goes through this process. Kubernetes would rather be slow to update than wrong about the state of the cluster, so it pays the coordination cost on every write.

Linearizability vs. Serializability: Untangling the Terms

The term “strong consistency” hides an important distinction between two guarantees that come from entirely different intellectual traditions.

As mentioned, linearizability comes from the distributed systems community. It’s about single operations on single objects. If we write a value and then read it, we see our write. If someone else’s write completed before our read started, we see their write too. The ordering of operations respects real-time. In other words, what physically happened first is reflected in what we observe.

On the other hand, Serializability is about transactions, which are groups of operations that may touch multiple objects. Serializability guarantees that even though transactions run concurrently, the result is the same as if they ran one after another in some serial order. The serial order, however, doesn’t have to match real-time. A transaction that started later could be ordered “before” an earlier one, as long as the final state is equivalent to some valid sequential execution. Serializability is the “I” (Isolation) in ACID (Atomicity, Consistency, Isolation, Durability), not the “C.”

This distinction has important consequences. We can have one without the other. A system can be serializable but not linearizable: transactions are properly isolated, but between transactions, we might read stale data. A system can be linearizable, but not serializable. In other words, individual reads and writes are always fresh, but multi-object transactions aren’t properly isolated from each other.

The strongest guarantee comes from combining both. When we add linearizability’s real-time ordering constraint to serializability, we get strict serializability, sometimes called external consistency. This means transactions execute as if serial, and the serial order respects real time. If transaction T1 finishes before T2 starts, T2 is guaranteed to see T1’s effects.

Google Spanner provides external consistency, and the way it achieves this is by using TrueTime, a clock system built on GPS receivers and atomic clocks deployed in every Google data center. TrueTime gives Spanner globally meaningful timestamps with bounded uncertainty (typically under a few milliseconds). When a transaction commits, Spanner performs a “commit wait,” pausing until the uncertainty window has passed, which guarantees that any future transaction will receive a higher timestamp.

CockroachDB, an open-source database inspired by Spanner, pursues the same goal without Google’s hardware advantage. It relies on software-based clock synchronization instead of dedicated GPS and atomic clock infrastructure. Their own engineers describe CockroachDB’s consistency model as stronger than serializability but slightly weaker than strict serializability, allowing a rare anomaly called “causal reverse.” It’s a pragmatic tradeoff: getting very close to the strongest guarantee without requiring atomic clocks.

The Real Cost: Latency, Availability, and Tradeoffs

Now that we understand what strong consistency promises and how it’s delivered, we need to understand what it costs. There are three costs, and all of them are fundamental.

1 - Latency

Every write must wait for quorum acknowledgment. In a single-region deployment, this adds a few milliseconds. In a multi-region deployment, it adds tens to hundreds of milliseconds per write, depending on the distance between replicas.

CockroachDB’s engineering team puts typical cross-country latency at 60-80ms round-trip, while cross-ocean connections (say, New York to London) exceed 80ms. These numbers aren’t the result of inefficient code or poor network configuration. They’re a consequence of the speed of light through fiber. No amount of engineering can make New York and Tokyo closer together.

2 - Availability

If a network partition splits a cluster so that no group of nodes can form a majority, the system stops accepting writes. It chooses unavailability over the risk of returning stale or contradictory data.

This is the CP side of the CAP theorem. In practice, the question is: how often do partitions actually happen?

For example, Eric Brewer (who formulated the CAP theorem) has described Google’s Spanner as “technically CP but effectively CA,” because partitions in Google’s network are rare enough that teams don’t even write code to handle them. However, that level of network reliability is an infrastructure privilege most organizations don’t have.

3 - The Three-Way Tradeoff

In a distributed database, we cannot simultaneously have all three of low-latency writes, survival of complete region failures, and strong consistency. We can pick any two.

  • If we want strong consistency with regional survivability, writes must cross regions for quorum, so we pay with latency.

  • If we want strong consistency with low-latency writes, we need to keep replicas in one region, but lose survivability if that region goes down.

  • If we want low latency and survivability, we’ll need to relax consistency.

There’s also a subtlety that the CAP framing misses. The PACELC framework extends CAP to capture the tradeoff during normal operation, not just during partitions. Even when the network is healthy, strongly consistent systems are choosing consistency over latency on every single write. The latency cost isn’t something we pay occasionally during failures. It’s the steady-state price of the guarantee.

When Is Strong Consistency Worth It?

Given these costs, how do developers actually use strong consistency?

The short answer is selectively. Most production systems don’t apply one consistency model to all data. They evaluate the consequences of inconsistency for each type of data and choose accordingly.

Strong consistency is typically worth the cost for financial transactions and ledgers, where two users seeing different balances creates real business and legal problems. It’s worth it for inventory and booking systems, where overselling means broken promises to customers. It’s essential for distributed coordination like leader election, configuration management, and service discovery. Systems like etcd and Consul use it because infrastructure decisions must be authoritative. And it’s worth it for any data where a brief period of disagreement between readers isn’t just inconvenient but actually dangerous or costly.

On the other hand, strong consistency is usually overkill for social media feeds, like counts, and view counters, where being a few seconds stale is invisible to users. It’s unnecessary for analytics ingestion, logging, and metrics pipelines, where throughput matters far more than per-write freshness. It’s often not needed for data that’s naturally partitioned by user or geography: if a user’s profile data is only ever accessed from one region, global consistency adds cost without benefit.

It’s also worth remembering that choosing eventual consistency doesn’t eliminate complexity. It moves it. With strong consistency, the database handles the hard coordination work, and our application code can simply trust that reads are fresh. With eventual consistency, the database is simpler and faster, but our application may now need to handle conflict resolution, read-your-writes logic, and compensating transactions. Neither choice is “simpler” in absolute terms. They place the burden differently.

Summary

In this article, we have looked at strong consistency in detail. Here are the key learning points in brief:

  • Strong consistency guarantees that every read returns the most recent write. From the client’s perspective, there appears to be only one copy of the data, even though multiple replicas exist.

  • This guarantee requires consensus: a protocol where a majority of replica nodes must acknowledge a write before it’s confirmed. Raft and Paxos are the two dominant consensus algorithm families.

  • Linearizability guarantees real-time ordering of single operations. Serializability guarantees that concurrent transactions behave as if executed sequentially. Combining both gives strict serializability, the strongest guarantee.

  • The latency cost of strong consistency is bounded by physics: cross-region coordination requires round-trips limited by the speed of light, adding tens to hundreds of milliseconds per write.

  • During network partitions, strongly consistent systems choose unavailability over stale reads. The PACELC framework extends CAP to capture the latency-consistency tradeoff during normal operation, not just during failures.

  • We cannot simultaneously achieve low-latency writes, regional survivability, and strong consistency. This is a fundamental constraint driven by physics and consensus math, not an engineering limitation.

  • Experienced engineers apply strong consistency selectively to financial data, inventory, bookings, and infrastructure coordination, while using weaker models for feeds, counters, analytics, and naturally partitioned data.
