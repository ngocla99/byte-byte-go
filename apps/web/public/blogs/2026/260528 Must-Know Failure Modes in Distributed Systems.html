Must-Know Failure Modes in Distributed Systems

What does it mean for a distributed system to be up?

On a single machine, the answer is straightforward, since a program is either running or it has crashed, and the line between the two is usually obvious from a stack trace.

Distributed systems are not so simple. Every server can report healthy while users are seeing errors, the whole system can be technically working but stuck in a state it cannot recover from on its own, and it can quietly serve wrong data while every dashboard glows green.

None of these may be because of bugs in the conventional sense. They are recurring failure patterns that have been showing up across systems for decades, with names, mechanisms, and standard ways of defending against them.

In this article, we will look at the most significant failure mode patterns in distributed systems and the standard approaches to deal with each of them.

Why Failures in Distributed Systems Are Different

A program running on a single machine has a relatively simple relationship with the truth of what is happening.

The CPU is either executing instructions or it isn’t, memory is either intact or has been corrupted, and a process is either alive or has crashed and been noticed by the operating system. When something goes wrong, the failure tends to produce error codes, stack traces, or core dumps that point clearly at what broke.

Distributed systems behave differently because they are groups of machines exchanging messages over an unreliable network. Three structural properties of that arrangement give rise to the kinds of failures that a single machine cannot produce on its own.

  • The first is information asymmetry. No single component sees the whole picture, so when a service sends a request and gets no response, it has no way to know whether the request was lost, processed, and the response was lost, or is still being worked on. The information about what is happening is scattered across machines, with each one holding only a partial view.

  • The second is feedback loops. Components react to what they see happening elsewhere in the system, with timeouts triggering retries, failures triggering failovers, and latency spikes triggering autoscaling events. Each reaction is usually correct in isolation, but together they couple the components and create paths through which problems can amplify.

  • The third is the independence problem. Redundancy is built on the assumption that two components are unlikely to fail at the same time, and that assumption can be more fragile than it looks. Two replicas in the same rack share their power supply, two services running the same kernel share its bugs, and two consumers of the same poisoned message will all crash on it.

These three properties are what give distributed failures their distinct character.

We will now look at the failure modes that emerge from them, with each one teaching something about how the properties play out in practice.

Network Partitions

A network partition is the simplest distributed failure to picture.

The connection between the two parts of the cluster fails, and each part can talk to itself but not across to the other. Inside each half, dashboards remain green, and the nodes are alive, with processes responding normally. Essentially, the system has simply been split into two systems that have no idea the other still exists.

The textbook version of a partition is a clean break, as if someone had unplugged a cable, but real-world partitions tend to be messier. Sometimes one direction of traffic works while the other silently drops packets, sometimes a switch reboots and connectivity returns in patchy bursts, and sometimes a burst of packet loss makes nodes think their peers are down for ten seconds before the network recovers as if nothing had happened.

Partial and intermittent partitions are far more common than clean ones, and they are often the hardest to diagnose because each side’s view of what is working remains internally consistent.

The standard mitigation is a quorum-based consensus protocol, where forward progress requires a majority of nodes to agree. Raft and Paxos are the algorithms most production systems borrow from. The tradeoff is that during a partition, only one side can hold the majority, and the minority side has to refuse writes if it wants to stay consistent.

Split-Brain

Split-brain is what happens when a partition meets a naive leader election protocol.

The two halves of the cluster lose contact with each other, each notices that the original leader has gone silent, and each elects a new leader of its own. The cluster now has two leaders, both serving writes and both convinced they are the legitimate primary.

For a while, the system appears to work as both halves accept requests and replicate them internally. Then the partition heals, the two halves reconnect, and the cluster discovers a problem it cannot solve on its own. The same row in the database now has two different values written by two different leaders, and there is no way to merge those histories without choosing which writes to discard.

The defense against split-brain has two layers:

  • Quorum-based leader election ensures that only the majority side of a partition can elect a new leader, so a minority partition cannot crown its own primary.

  • Fencing tokens take this further by issuing each new leader an ever-increasing number, with any work submitted by a deposed leader rejected once a higher-numbered one exists. Even if the old leader is still alive and unaware that it has been deposed, its write operations are stopped before they can cause damage.

Split-brain can also happen without a network partition. If a leader pauses for thirty seconds during a long garbage collection cycle, its peers may declare it dead and elect a successor while the original is moments away from waking up and resuming work. From the cluster’s perspective, a long enough pause looks identical to a partition.

Partial Failures

It is tempting to read a timeout as a failure, but the reality is more ambiguous.

A timeout means something went wrong somewhere in the round trip, and four very different possibilities all look identical from outside. The request may never have arrived in the first place, the server may have crashed before processing it, the work may have completed with only the response getting lost on the way back, or the server may still be working and on the verge of replying.

This matters because the natural response to a failure, which is to retry, can be dangerous when we don’t actually know whether the operation failed. Consider a payment service where a timeout on the charge request might mean the money never moved, making a retry the correct fix, or it might mean the money moved and only the receipt was lost, making a retry the cause of a double charge.

The standard mitigation is idempotency. The client generates a unique request ID for each operation and sends it along with the request, so that if the server has already processed an operation carrying that ID, it can return the original result instead of doing the work again.

Gray Failures

Gray failure is one of the more counterintuitive modes to internalize.

A node is alive, responds to pings, reports its own health, passes every internal check the operations team has set up, and yet it is failing at the actual job it is supposed to do.

The classic case is a disk whose writes succeed but take thirty seconds to complete. The process is alive, the disk reports healthy at the OS level, and the health checks that don’t directly exercise the disk all return green. But every real query that touches the disk is timing out, and downstream services are watching their own success rates collapse because of one slow neighbor.

There are many ways for a node to be alive while broken:

  • A network card might drop packets in only one direction, so the node can send but increasingly cannot receive.

  • A process might fall into a long garbage collection pause and stop responding for ten seconds at a stretch.

  • A configuration change might subtly corrupt the responses while the status code stays at 200.

  • A memory leak might starve the cache so much that hit rates collapse without the process ever crashing.

Health checks miss these failures because they measure what they were designed to measure. A check that pings the process tells us the process is running, but it does not tell us whether the process is actually doing useful work.

The fix is observability that probes the actual workload rather than the metadata, through approaches like synthetic transactions that exercise the real code path, peer-based detection where neighboring nodes watch each other’s behavior, and end-to-end latency measurements taken from the customer’s perspective.

Cascading Failures

The previous failure modes had something in common. They were all about the system not knowing or not agreeing on something fundamental. The remaining failure modes work differently.

In each of them, the system can clearly see that something has gone wrong, takes action to address it, and that action is what turns a small problem into a much larger one. Many of the worst distributed failures aren’t caused by the initial trigger. They are caused by what the system does in response.

Retries, autoscaling, failover, load balancing, and health-driven traffic shifts are all responses, each one reacting to a signal from somewhere else in the system. Each of these mechanisms can be the right thing to do in isolation, but each can also, under the wrong conditions, become a feedback loop that amplifies a small problem into a full outage.

The most familiar instance of this pattern is the cascading failure, which begins with one component going down and ends with half the dependency graph on fire.

The mechanism is simple to understand. A single node fails, its load redirects to its peers, and those peers now find themselves carrying more traffic than they were provisioned for. As tail latency grows and some of them start to time out, their load redirects again to the remaining nodes, which become even more overloaded under the new pressure. By the time the on-call engineer reaches the dashboard, the original failure has run out of hand.

One of the more counterintuitive aspects of cascading failures is that adding more capacity does not always help. Putting more nodes into rotation during a cascade simply gives the failure more places to spread before backpressure kicks in.

The reliable fix is to break propagation rather than try to absorb it. Circuit breakers do this by stopping calls to a struggling service so that callers fail fast instead of piling on, while backpressure pushes overload signals upstream so producers slow down. Load shedding drops a fraction of incoming requests so the system can protect what it is still able to serve, and bulkheads partition resources so that a failure in one tenant or region cannot consume the capacity meant for others.

Retry Storms

Retry storms are the cleanest example of the response acting as a failure. A server slows down or starts returning errors, and clients begin to retry because that is what clients do when a failure looks transient. Each client retrying in isolation is reasonable behavior, but many clients doing the same thing at the same time is not.

The arithmetic of this can get problematic quite fast. For example, if every client retries three times after a failure, the load on a struggling server triples at exactly the moment when it most needs traffic to drop. The problem compounds when retries stack across layers, where a client may have its own retry logic, the libraries it depends on may also retry, and the gateway sitting in front of everything may retry on top of all that.

Two additions to retry logic largely prevent this:

  • Exponential backoff increases the waiting time between retries so that a single client doesn’t keep hammering

  • Jitter adds a random offset to each wait so that many clients don’t all retry at the same instant. Without jitter, exponential backoff merely synchronizes the storm to a different timeline.

The pattern shows up routinely in production incidents, where a database starts to recover, the queued retries hit it together, and it falls over again. A retry budget caps the total retry traffic the system will tolerate before refusing further retries entirely.

Thundering Herd

In the thundering herd, the trigger is synchronization itself. Many processes wake up at the same instant and converge on a single resource, with each one doing something perfectly normal in isolation. The damage comes entirely from the fact that they all arrive at the same moment.

The most common version of this is the cache stampede. For example, a popular cache key expires, and every web server that depended on it tries to regenerate it from the database at almost exactly the same moment. The database, which had been comfortably serving the cached version’s miss rate, suddenly receives thousands of identical queries within a few hundred milliseconds. Other variants follow the same general pattern, including cron jobs scheduled on the same minute, mass client reconnection after a service restart, and the coordinated retries that make retry storms a special case of the same dynamic.

The mitigation in every case is desynchronization. Jitter spreads arrivals across a small window so that they no longer line up, while request coalescing allows the first request to kick off the work and queues the others to wait for its result, turning a thousand identical requests into one. Locking around regeneration plays a similar role by ensuring that only one worker rebuilds the cache while the rest wait.

Metastable Failures

Metastable failure is the mode that defeats the primary debugging instinct of finding the root cause and fixing it. That instinct works on most failures, but it does not work on this one.

A metastable failure starts with a trigger, which could be a traffic spike, a deployment, or a momentarily slow database. The trigger pushes the system into a degraded state, and once the system is in that state, its own behavior keeps it there. Requests start timing out, clients begin retrying, and the retries keep the system overloaded. Even after the original trigger is gone, the system stays overloaded because the retries don’t stop, because the requests keep timing out, because the system is still overloaded.

What makes this mode dangerous is the inversion of cause and effect. The cause that started the incident is no longer present, and removing it changes nothing because the system has settled into a different equilibrium and will stay there until someone breaks the feedback loop directly.

The mitigation is to break the loop directly. Load is shed aggressively so the system has fewer requests to fail, traffic is dropped at the edge until the queue drains, and in the worst cases, components are restarted so that all in-flight work is wiped clean. None of these addresses the original cause, since the original cause is no longer present.

Each of the previous three amplification modes can create or sustain a metastable state. A retry storm that doesn’t subside on its own is essentially metastable, and a cascade that stabilizes at a degraded throughput is too. Metastable failure is the worst-case form that the amplification family can take.

Correlated Failures

Every mitigation discussed so far rests on the assumption that failures are independent of one another. For example, quorum protocols assume that a majority of nodes won’t fail at the same time, replication across regions assumes that regions don’t share a failure mode, and retries to a sibling assume that the peer isn’t broken in the same way as the original. The math of redundancy works when this assumption holds.

Correlated failures are the cases where this assumption breaks:

  • Three replicas in the same rack share a power supply, and a power event takes all of them out at the same instant.

  • Five servers running the same kernel version share that kernel’s bugs, and a triggering input crashes all five.

  • A queue with redundant consumers receives a single poisoned message, and every consumer that processes it crashes on it.

The redundancy was real on paper, but the failures, in practice, were not independent.

Mitigation requires designing the system so that failure domains stay small and don’t share more than they need to. Cell-based architectures isolate customers or workloads into separate cells so that a failure in one cell cannot take down the others, while diverse infrastructure spreads risk across different availability zones, kernel versions, and network paths. Blast radius reduction is the deliberate choice to limit how much of the system any single failure can take down with it.

Summary

In this article, we’ve looked at the key failure modes in detail. Here are the key learning points in brief:

  • Network partitions split the cluster into groups that can talk internally but not across, while each side’s view of the world stays internally consistent.

  • Split-brain happens when both halves of a partition elect their own leader and accept conflicting writes that cannot be cleanly merged when the partition heals.

  • Partial failures are the cases where a request times out and the system cannot tell whether it failed, succeeded with a lost response, or is still in flight.

  • Gray failures occur when a node passes its own health checks while quietly failing at the work it is actually supposed to do.

  • Cascading failures happen when one component goes down, its load shifts to its siblings, and the redirected traffic overwhelms them in turn.

  • Retry storms occur when many clients react to a slow service by retrying at once, multiplying the load just as the server needs it to drop.

  • Thundering herds form when many processes wake up at the same instant and converge on one resource, regardless of how reasonable each one is in isolation.

  • Metastable failures occur when the system stays broken even after the original trigger is gone, because its own behavior keeps it stuck in the bad state.

  • Correlated failures are the cases where redundancy buys nothing because the components were never actually independent in the way the design assumed.

  • Many of the worst failures are not caused by the trigger but by what the system does in response, which is why retries, failover, and autoscaling all need careful design.

  • The control plane that elects leaders and triggers recovery is itself a distributed system, so when it fails, the safety nets that rely on it fail along with it.

  • Most real outages are two or three of these modes stacked on top of each other rather than one in isolation, which is part of why postmortems read the way they do.
